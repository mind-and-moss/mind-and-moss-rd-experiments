# Decomposers Beyond Isopods and Springtails

> **Convention note:** This file is **external research synthesis**, not AI-conversation excavation like most other files in `topics/`. Sources are real primary references (peer-reviewed papers, university extension publications, established hobbyist references) cited inline with `Source:` lines pointing to the author + URL. If a claim is unverified or hobbyist-anecdote, it's flagged as such. Companion files: `soil-chemistry.md`, `microorganisms.md`.

Isopods and springtails get the credit, but they're not alone. A mature bioactive enclosure — especially anything bigger than a 20-gallon — supports a fuller decomposer community: millipedes, predatory mites, beetles, nematodes, and (at the paludarium scale) earthworms. This file covers them in roughly the order you'd consider adding them, and in each case how they fit into the "permanent bioactive — no disassembly needed" loop documented in `bioactive-systems.md` (RESEARCH-001 Thread 3).

## Millipedes

Millipedes are dedicated detritivores. Unlike isopods, which scavenge a mix of plant matter, fungi, and the occasional protein, millipedes eat almost exclusively decaying wood and leaf litter — and they specifically prefer it once **bacteria and fungi have started conditioning it** (Welcome Wildlife / animaldiversity.org). They're slow, they don't climb glass, and they almost never become a pest.

**Three species commonly available to the U.S. hobby:**

- **Narceus americanus / N. gordanus** ("American giant millipede") — eastern North American native, 4 inches long, slate-gray with red bands. Wild-collected legally across the southeast. Lives in the soil-litter interface; especially important as a **calcium cycler** — they have unusually high body calcium and turn over Ca through their casting (Animal Diversity Web).
- **Chicobolus spinigerus** ("ivory millipede") — Florida native, similar care, smaller and lighter colored.
- **Anadenobolus monilicornis** ("bumblebee millipede") — Caribbean species, established as a non-native in south Florida since 2001; controversial — invasive in Florida but a popular hobby species elsewhere. Smaller (~10 cm), more humidity-tolerant, breeds readily in captivity.

For a tropical bioactive viv with isopods and springtails, bumblebee millipedes coexist well. They eat roughly the same diet as the isopods (decayed wood, leaf litter, occasional protein) but operate at a slower pace and a slightly different size class — competition is minimal.

- Source: Lam et al. 2024, *Functional Ecology* — *Effects of leaf litter traits on terrestrial isopod and millipede consumption* — https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1365-2435.14520
- Source: Joulian et al. — *Detritivore conversion of litter into faeces accelerates organic matter turnover* (PMC) — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7658975/

## Predatory Mites

Mites in a vivarium fall into three groups:

1. **Soil/grain mites** — usually harmless detritivores. Visible as tiny white or tan dots on glass. If you see population blooms it usually signals overfeeding.
2. **Pest mites on the inhabitants** (snake mites, lizard mites). Different problem; not a decomposer issue.
3. **Predatory mites** — beneficial, used deliberately.

The key beneficial species is **Stratiolaelaps scimitus** (formerly *Hypoaspis miles*). It lives in the top inch of substrate, hunts fungus gnat larvae, sciarid larvae, thrips pupae, and several pest-mite species. A single mite can consume up to ~30 fungus gnat larvae per day. They establish a permanent population from a single inoculation if the substrate stays damp — exactly matching the "no-disassembly bioactive" principle.

They do **not** harm isopods, springtails, millipedes, or anything bigger than ~1 mm. Kansas State Extension (Cloyd, MF3632) is the canonical North American extension reference.

- Source: Cloyd, R. — *Stratiolaelaps scimitus: Biological Control Agent of Fungus Gnats and the Western Flower Thrips*, KSRE MF3632 — https://bookstore.ksre.ksu.edu/download/stratiolaelaps-scimitus-biological-control-agent-of-fungus-gnats-and-the-western-flower-thrips_MF3632
- Source: Berndt et al. 2003, *Biological Control* (PubMed) — https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16082925/

## Earthworms

Earthworms are too big for nano- and small terrariums but become viable in a paludarium or any enclosure with at least 4–6 inches of soil. Castings have 5–11× the N, P, and K of surrounding soil (UF/IFAS). They aerate substrate and accelerate organic matter turnover.

**Species choice matters:**

- *Eisenia fetida* (red wiggler) — epigeic, lives in the litter layer, perfect for a paludarium with deep leaf litter.
- *Eisenia hortensis* (European nightcrawler) — slightly larger, similar habits, more cold-tolerant.
- *Lumbricus terrestris* (common nightcrawler) — anecic, deep-burrowing, makes vertical tunnels several feet long. **Not suitable** for closed enclosures because they need much more depth than any tank provides.

For Mind and Moss-scale builds: red wigglers in a paludarium with at least 4 inches of substrate are a quiet workhorse and rarely visible.

- Source: UF/IFAS Extension — *Vermicomposting (Using Worms for Composting)* — https://blogs.ifas.ufl.edu/orangeco/2020/12/10/vermicomposting-using-worms-for-composting/
- Source: New Mexico State University Extension H-164 — *Vermicomposting* — https://pubs.nmsu.edu/_h/H164/

## Detritivore Beetles

Adult beetles in a vivarium are usually a problem (escape risk, dietary needs not matching enclosure conditions), but several **larval forms** function as detritivores:

- **Mealworms (*Tenebrio molitor* larvae)** and **superworms (*Zophobas morio* larvae)** are darkling beetle juveniles. They eat dry detritus — bran, dried leaves, dog food. In a humid bioactive viv they tend to die or pupate; they're better as a feeder culture in a separate dry tub than an in-tank decomposer.
- **Lesser mealworms (*Alphitobius diaperinus*)** tolerate higher humidity and have been used in some hobby setups, but they can get into reptile food and become a pest.

The honest answer: for typical bioactive setups, beetles are not where you focus. Isopods + millipedes cover the same niche better.

## Nematodes

Soil nematodes (microscopic, 0.1–1 mm worms) are present in every healthy substrate. The community includes:

- **Bacterivores** — the dominant group. Eat bacteria, mineralize nitrogen as a side effect. Beneficial.
- **Fungivores** — graze fungal hyphae, similar role to springtails.
- **Predatory nematodes** — eat other nematodes and protozoa.
- **Plant-parasitic nematodes** (root-knot, cyst nematodes) — pests, generally absent from sterilized commercial substrates and rarely a vivarium issue unless you bring contaminated outdoor soil in.
- **Entomopathogenic nematodes** — predators of insect larvae, used commercially.

The most commonly used commercial entomopathogenic species is **Steinernema feltiae**, sold as NemaShield, NemAttack, Nemasys, and Scanmask. The nematode enters fungus gnat larvae through natural openings, releases a symbiotic *Xenorhabdus* bacterium that produces a toxin, and the larva dies in 24–48 hours. UMass Extension confirms efficacy and gives application rates.

S. feltiae is functionally a **redundant tool** if you already have *Stratiolaelaps* established — both target fungus gnat larvae. Nematodes work faster on an active outbreak; mites work better as a permanent presence.

- Source: UMass Extension — *Biological Control: Using Beneficial Nematodes* — https://www.umass.edu/agriculture-food-environment/greenhouse-floriculture/fact-sheets/biological-control-using-beneficial-nematodes
- Source: UConn IPM — *Beneficial Nematodes Fact Sheet* — https://ipm.cahnr.uconn.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/3216/2022/12/2019beneficialnematodesfactsheetffinal-2.pdf

## Fungus Gnat Larvae — Pest or Decomposer?

Fungus gnats (Sciaridae) are technically detritivores: their larvae eat fungi, decaying plant matter, and root tips. In low numbers they cause no harm. The problem is that vivarium conditions — damp, organic-rich, no overwintering — let them breed unchecked, and the adults are a nuisance even if the larvae are harmless.

Standard control sequence:

1. Let *Stratiolaelaps scimitus* establish from day one. They handle background populations indefinitely.
2. If a bloom happens anyway (often after adding new soil or wet wood), apply *S. feltiae* nematodes as a drench. Knocks the larval population down in a week.
3. Sticky yellow cards trap adults but don't fix the breeding cycle.

The "permanent bioactive — no disassembly" insight from RESEARCH-001 Thread 3 lines up here: the right initial inoculation (mites + nematodes, even just once) sets up perpetual self-regulation. You don't have to remove substrate, sterilize, or rebuild — the predators handle the prey indefinitely as long as the moisture is in range.

## How They All Fit Together

Layering the cast over a single enclosure:

| Niche | Primary | Secondary / Backup |
|---|---|---|
| Surface plant-litter shredding | Isopods | Millipedes |
| Fungal grazing | Springtails | Fungivore nematodes |
| Bacterial mineralization | Bacterivore nematodes | Heterotrophic bacteria (always present) |
| Fungus-gnat suppression | *Stratiolaelaps* mites | *Steinernema feltiae* nematodes (on demand) |
| Deep substrate turnover (paludarium scale) | Earthworms (red wigglers) | Larger millipedes |

Add the first three rows on day one. Add the predatory mites at first signs of fungus gnats — or pre-emptively if you have the budget. Earthworms are an option only at paludarium scale. Beetles you can skip.

## Open Questions

- **Unverified**: long-term coexistence outcomes of *Anadenobolus monilicornis* + *Stratiolaelaps* + Powder Orange isopods in a single 20-gallon enclosure — anecdotal hobbyist reports are positive but I found no formal study.
- **Unverified for vivarium scale**: whether *Steinernema feltiae* applications need to be repeated, or whether they self-establish like mites do. Greenhouse literature suggests they do not persist long-term in dry-cycle settings; in a constantly damp viv the answer may differ. Worth a Mind and Moss observational test.
- **Legal**: bumblebee millipedes (*Anadenobolus monilicornis*) are a regulated invasive in some Florida counties — verify shipping/possession laws before stocking customer enclosures in FL.
