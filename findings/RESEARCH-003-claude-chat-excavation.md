## Thread 10: 3D Print Prep for Clay Application (The Gem — fabrication workflow)
**Conversation:** "Preparing 3D prints for clay application"
**Date:** Apr 29

### Context
Isaiah described his full workflow: 3D model → 3D print → clay over print → texture → shellac seal → silicone + plaster mold. Asked specifically what steps to take between the print and the clay.

### Workflow: Between Print and Clay

1. **Remove supports and clean up**
   - Pull off support material, trim artifacts/blobs with flush cutters or hobby knife

2. **Sand the print**
   - Even when clay goes over it, surface affects how clay sits and behaves
   - Work through grits: 120 to start, up to 220
   - Don't need perfect finish — just knock down worst layer lines so they don't telegraph through thin clay sections

3. **Seal the print (critical step)**
   - Oil-based clays (Chavant, Monster Clay, etc.) contain plasticizers that actively attack and degrade PLA/ABS over time
   - Plasticizers soften/warp the print under the clay while you're working
   - Need a barrier: shellac, spray lacquer, or filler primer
   - Shellac is already in Isaiah's workflow — works perfectly

### Disposable vs Reusable Armature
Isaiah confirmed: **disposable** (one-time mold master, print gets trashed after)

**For disposable:**
- Light sand (just knock down worst layer lines, quick pass)
- Seal (2 coats spray lacquer or filler primer — fastest option; shellac also works)
- No release layer needed since print stays inside permanently

**For reusable (not Isaiah's case, documented for reference):**
- Sand + seal same way
- Add petroleum jelly (Vaseline) or paste wax on top of shellac as release layer
- Vaseline easier to work into details than paste wax
- Watch for severe undercuts — Vaseline won't save you, may need to cut print apart

### Isaiah's Final Sequence (permanent clay, print as armature)
Print → light sand → shellac → clay + texture → shellac over clay → silicone + plaster mold

No Vaseline. No release layer. The print lives inside as the armature permanently. Clean and simple.

### Clay Adhesion to Shellac
- Yes — oil-based clay sticks to shellac during working
- Shellac dries to slightly porous, matte-to-satin surface that clay grips reasonably well
- Shellac protects print from plasticizers; clay grips shellac fine for sculpting

### Shellac vs UV Resin for Sealing 3D Prints

Shellac:
- Clay adhesion: Good (porous, matte-satin surface)
- Hardness: Moderate, slightly flexible — won't crack if print has any give
- Application: Brush or spray, fast, multiple thin coats build well
- Already in Isaiah's workflow
- Downside: not the hardest surface, can reactivate with alcohol

UV Resin:
- Clay adhesion: Poor (glass-smooth surface — clay slides off)
- Fix: sand after curing to give clay something to grip
- Hardness: Rock hard, like a glass coating
- Application: Needs UV lamp for even curing on 3D print
- Cost: More expensive
- Better for: Display pieces where you want a hard final surface

Winner for Isaiah's workflow: SHELLAC. UV resin's glass-smooth surface works against clay adhesion.
