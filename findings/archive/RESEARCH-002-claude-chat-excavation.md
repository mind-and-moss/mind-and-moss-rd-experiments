## Thread 4: Cylindrical Terrarium — Film Installation Research
**Conversation:** "Foggy window liner for sealed terrariums" (April 16)

### The Tank
Isaiah has a sealed cylindrical acrylic terrarium with top and bottom plastic stands. Established bioactive ecosystem (springtails, timed light). He wants foggy window film on the INSIDE walls, top to bottom to the plastic joint corner.

### Routes Compared (A-F)
- Route A: Inside-mount film + aquarium silicone corner seal. 3-5 year lifespan. Isaiah's plan. Film edge is the long-term weak point.
- Route B: Outside-mount film. Indefinite lifespan. Trivially replaceable, zero humidity exposure. Different optical look.
- Route C: Adhesive-backed frosted film, inside. Unknown durability. Adhesive chemistry unvetted for sealed bioactive environments.
- Route D: Paint/frost exterior. 10+ years. Permanent, limited by UV fade from grow light.
- Route E: Etched/sandblasted acrylic. Permanent. Pre-purchase only — not retrofittable.
- Route F: Inside-mount + neutral-cure silicone or vivarium foam. Bonds better to plastic but different off-gas profile (methanol/oximes).

### Key Technical Notes
- Safe silicone: 100% silicone, no mildewcide/fungicide (GE Silicone I, Aqueon, ASI). NOT GE Silicone II (contains mildewcide).
- Acrylic care: No isopropyl, acetone, or ammonia. Use mild soap + water or Novus #1.
- Cylinder geometry advantage: Silicone corner bead sits in a 90-degree mechanical pocket — more durable than a flat perimeter seal on a rectangular tank.
- Cure protocol with fans: Film dry-out 3-5 days → lightly sand film edges → lay silicone bead → cure 7-10 more days until zero vinegar smell.
- Isaiah's setup advantage: Has 24/7 mini fans and can wait 2 weeks. This eliminates both main failure risks (off-gassing, trapped moisture behind film).

## Thread 5: Clay Substrates + Stained Glass + Glass Fusing (The Gem)
**Conversation:** "Clay substrates for terrariums" (April 22, long multi-topic thread)

### Part A: Clay Substrate Types
Context: Isaiah cross-referencing ome.design terrarium putty recipe ("terrarium clay powder" = likely bentonite + kaolin blend).

- Bentonite clay powder: waterproof barrier, swells when wet, used in putty. Sodium bentonite cat litter (unscented, no additives) is a cheap source.
- Calcined clay: resists compaction, wicks moisture upward toward roots.
- LECA: lightweight expanded clay aggregate, creates air gap, prevents drowned roots.
- Fresh Step cat litter: NOT usable. Ammonia blockers + activated carbon are inseparable from the clay — cannot be removed.
- These three do different jobs and are not interchangeable.

### Part B: Stained Glass Soldering & Polishing
- Iron: temperature-controlled at ~700-750F (Hakko FX-601 or Weller 100W). Tin seam first, then run bead in one smooth pass per line.
- Solder: 60/40 (tin/lead) flows smoother. Lead-free (silver-bearing) achievable after 5-10 pieces of practice.
- Polishing: 400-grit wet/dry → 600 → 1000 → 1500 → 2000, used wet with dish soap. Work along the seam, not across it.
- Patina: lead-free doesn't take Novacan Black the same way — gives gunmetal not deep black. Copper patina works fine.

### Part C: Glass Fusing for Sphere/Hemisphere — Direct Gem Research
Isaiah explored making a fused glass sphere or hemisphere (the light-scattering gem component of The Gem).

Kiln requirements:
- Small (6-8" spheres): Evenheat Studio Pro STP or Paragon SC-2 — ~$700-1200
- Medium (10-12"): Skutt GM1014 or Evenheat GTS — ~$2000-3000
- Look for "glass kiln" (heats from top), not "ceramic kiln" (heats from sides). Used kilns on Facebook Marketplace often half price.

COE compatibility — critical:
- Bullseye (COE 90) — most popular for art glass, huge color range
- System 96 / Spectrum / Uroboros (COE 96) — also common
- Pick one system and stay there. Mixing COEs = guaranteed cracking during cooling.
- Traditional stained glass cathedral sheets are COE 82-85 — NOT fusing-compatible. Different product entirely.

Slump molds for hemisphere shape:
- Buy: Bullseye, Slumpy's, Creative Paradise sell ceramic hemisphere molds 6"-12", ~$40-150. Best starting point.
- DIY: 50/50 plaster + silica blend, cast around a ball. Advanced, can fail if not dried fully.

### Part D: UV Resin Over Solder (For Submerged Components)
- What it does: seals tarnish, waterproofs seam pinholes by capillary action, locks patina permanently.
- Weakness: UV resin yellows under sustained UV (ironic given the name).
- Mitigation: optical-grade UV adhesives (e.g. Norland) maintain clarity. Silicone is inherently UV-stable.
- Indoor terrarium grow lights = moderate UV exposure, mid-grade UV adhesives last years.
- Outdoor or window-exposed = need UV-stabilized formulations specifically.

## Thread 6: The Gem Full Construction Deep-Dive
**Conversation:** "3D CAD software for hobbyists" (massive multi-topic thread — CAD was just the opening question)

### Part A: Seam/Joinery Methods — Ranked by Achievable + Visual Impact
1. UV optical adhesive seams — most likely to make viewers ask "how is this built?" Invisible seams, glass appears bonded by nothing.
2. Hybrid: solder inside / optical adhesive outside — structural integrity + visually invisible from outside.
3. Embedded side-glow fiber — accessible, dramatic, photographs beautifully.

### Part B: Base Material — Mycelium Composite Concept
Standard approach: concrete base supporting the bottom quarter of the glass structure.

Isaiah's discovery: mycelium-bound hempcrete or cork composite as the base.
- Base IS the substrate — living/breathing material and structural element simultaneously.
- Mycelium composites: hobbyist-accessible, cure at room temperature, light, strong, shapeable in molds.
- Aesthetic: organic textured base under crystalline glass top.
- Key observation: "The stained-glass and terrarium worlds haven't crossed paths with the biomaterials world yet." This is a differentiation opportunity.

### Part C: Glass Cutting & Marking Technique
- Recommendation: 0.3mm Sharpie Ultra-Fine (black for clear glass), white paint marker for tinted/textured glass. Steel ruler/square as straightedge.
- Pro tip: Don't mark the cut line itself. Mark parallel ~5mm offset. Cutting fluid + cutter wheel crossing a Sharpie line can drag pigment into the score, contaminating the break.
- Avoid: standard Sharpies (too wide), ballpoint pens (won't mark glass), graphite (slips off), dry-erase (wipes on touch).
- Layout workflow: mark corners only (small + or x marks) → score one cut at a time between corner marks → wipe each panel clean after cutting before stacking.

### Part D: Glass Grinding Equipment
No-compromise setup recommendation:
- Used Diamond Pacific Tech Master 8" (~$800-1200 used) — stages 3-5 on panels up to 18"
- Keep existing stained-glass grinder — stages 1-2 and curve work
- DIY belt grinder with diamond belts — for occasional large panels (24"+)
- Total: ~$1000-1500 used + DIY
- If DIY ends up handling everything, sell the commercial machine and recoup most of the cost.

### Part E: Resin Types for Seams
- UV resin (Solarez, Padico, Counter Culture DIY UV): cures in seconds, controlled flow, $15-30/bottle. Good for precise seam application.
- Polyester resin: strong odor, traditional, used in fiberglass work.
- Doming / glazing resin: self-leveling, glass-clear, thin topcoat. "Probably what's being used for the look you're describing."
- Process: build piece → clean seams (alcohol wipe, dust removal) → tape glass faces adjacent to each seam to prevent drips → mix + apply resin → cure.

### Part F: UV Adhesive Stability
- Standard UV adhesives without UV stabilizers: yellow visibly in 30 days of direct sunlight.
- Optical-grade UV adhesives (Norland): maintain clarity long-term.
- Silicone: inherently UV-stable, won't yellow.
- Indoor terrarium grow lights = mid-grade UV adhesives last years.
- Outdoor or window-exposed = UV-stabilized formulations specifically required.

### Part G: Silicone Cure Inhibition (For Mold-Making)
Platinum-cure silicone will not cure if it contacts:
- Latex or natural rubber (gloves, rubber bands)
- Sulfur-containing clays (Plastilina, Roma Plastilina, most oil-based clays)
- Tin-cure silicone residue on tools or containers
- Certain spray paints, lacquers, polyester resins
- Uncured SLA resin from 3D-printed masters (must be fully post-cured before molding)
- Superglue / CA glue residue

## Thread 7: Clay-Coated 3D Print — Earliest Concept Version
**Conversation:** "Clay-coated 3D print with UV resin terrarium display" — April 19 instance (2-turn, abandoned)

Isaiah's original concept statement:
"3D print the form → coat with clay → paint → UV resin topcoat → place in terrarium for years under 8hr lighting. No acrylic because I want to modify later."

Claude identified the tension: UV resin is permanent once cured — "changing things" becomes impossible at that point. Three options presented:
1. Long working window on the paint layer (rework before sealing); UV resin is still the final topcoat.
2. Pop piece out months or years later and genuinely repaint — topcoat must be removable.
3. Something else.

Conversation was abandoned before Isaiah answered. This is the seed concept that was later resolved in RESEARCH-001 Thread 3 — the full workflow became: 3D print → polymer clay → shellac seal → silicone mold → cast final piece (print never enters the tank).
