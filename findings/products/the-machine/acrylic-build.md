# The Machine — Acrylic Build (sealing tiers + invisible internal structures)

How acrylic is welded, sealed, and used as invisible internal structure in The Machine. Specific to the thin-acrylic + concrete-hybrid construction approach.

---

Source: RESEARCH-005 Thread 16 — "Acrylic Aquarium Build Tips" (chat #14)

## Material reality check
- **0.04" (1mm) acrylic is too thin** for anything taller than 2-3" of water column.
- Fine for: tiny micro tanks, humidity chambers, lids, baffles.
- Risky for: any tank with real water column.

## Sealing tier system

**Tier 1 — flawless solvent weld**
- No glue layer at all
- Solvent melts both edges, polymer chains interdiffuse, seam becomes one continuous sheet
- As strong as parent material
- Adding silicone does almost nothing structurally on top of this

**Tier 2 — weld + internal silicone fillet**
- Backup against real-world imperfections (microscopic bubbles, tiny dry spots, slight edge mismatch, stress risers)
- Tier 2 is BELOW Tier 1 because it implies the weld wasn't perfect or the build is risky (thin material)

**Why other sealants don't work as primary structure:**
Silicone, boat sealants, and most resins are NOT reliable structural seals for acrylic. They can help as secondary insurance only.

## 5-year lifespan target
- Discussed throughout
- Algae environment effect on silicone considered
- Black aquarium silicone preferred for visible seams

## Edge techniques (silicone application)
- Sandpaper the silicone area before applying
- Tape, razor blade, fingertip techniques compared
- **Acrylic cutter for "hook" cuts** to mechanically anchor silicone discussed

## Invisible internal acrylic structures (KEY for The Machine aesthetic)
- Acrylic refractive index ≈ 1.49 vs water ≈ 1.33 — close enough that flat clean acrylic disappears underwater
- **Visibility killers:** polished glowing edges, surface scratches, algae film
- Design goal: control all three
- Structural ideas: hidden shelves, plant ledges, floating dividers, shrimp grazing platforms, moss terraces
- **Pro move:** place under driftwood shadows
- Flat horizontal acrylic virtually disappears underwater when thin, clean, shadowed from above

## Concrete + acrylic hybrid (10-gallon viewing tank pattern)
- True unsupported 10-gallon wall CANNOT be safely made from 1mm acrylic for 5-year service
- Pressure: ~0.43 psi per foot at the bottom of a 12" tank
- **Winning strategy:** "Let the concrete carry the load, let the acrylic act as windows"
- Concrete = pressure vessel, acrylic = viewport
- This is how large public aquariums think (scaled down)

## Acrylic-to-concrete bonding
- Different game from acrylic-to-acrylic — flex-tolerant gasket joint between two very different materials
- Acrylic: flexible, smooth, low surface energy
- Concrete: rigid, porous, slightly moving with moisture
- **Aquarium silicone wins** — flexible, tolerates differential movement, excellent water resistance, bonds well to porous concrete, easy to service, long track record
- Public aquariums often use silicone gaskets even with acrylic windows

## Best frame materials for acrylic
1. **Glass** — silicone bonds chemically to glass. Joint very reliable even though silicone only mechanically grips the acrylic side.
2. **PVC sheet** — slightly flexible (matches acrylic), doesn't wick water, machines easily, underrated.
3. **Concrete** — rigid, porous. Works for the bottom only (where rigidity is fine).
